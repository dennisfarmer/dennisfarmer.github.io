To the surprise of literally no one, COVID-19 had put quite the spell on every person's mental health. Everyone I know jokes about how unproductive they have been, and how they waste so much time on video games, Netflix, and sleeping (the latter of which is a popular symptom of depression!).

All of the hobbies that I had been putting time into slowly dwindled away into frustration and procrastination. My skills at marimba, guitar, and programming / data analysis have all taken a massive dive, as well as my exercise, meditation, and journaling habits. To combat this, I have researched a number of strategies to counteract the lethargic cloud of COVID-19 mental health.

# The 2 Day Rule

> > <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bfLHTLQZ5nc" target="_blank">Matt D'Avella: "If we never set rules for ourself, we're simply letting our current feelings, desires, and mood move our path out of allignment."</a>

Many studies say that it can take an average of two months to start a new habit. In today's scatterbrained society, commiting to doing a specific task consistantly over a number of weeks can prove to be nearly impossible.

The 2 day rule is a simple way to build habits and stay on track, while still allowing for human imperfections to exist. The rule is simple: never skip 2 days of a habit in a row. Occasional off-days are fine, but the habit in question must be performed at the bare minimum of every other day. Over the period of a few months, this simple rule can allow for an otherwise unmotivated person to keep habits that can help them improve themselves.

# Frequent and Effective Journaling

> > <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rfeADdY1rHw" target="_blank">Nathaniel Drew: "The point of this isn't to beat yourself up and try to suddenly be perfect. Currently, I'm just assesing the situation and trying to bring as much curiosity to this as possible, almost as if I were doing a little experiment."</a>

There exists so, so, so many different ways to journal your thoughts and stuff, but the most important thing in self-improvement journaling is to be consistent and useful. Nathaniel Drew's bullet journaling technique is likely what I'll end up adapting into my own journaling style, but different people will benefit from other methods.

For example, while to-do lists work wonders for some people, I find that for me they are not a viable way to get things done, and are better replaced with productivity principals like prioritizing and allocating energy over multiple days (splitting tasks up and avoiding procrastination).

When first starting out, consider writing a few sentences on how your day went, including struggles you faced and patterns you noticed in yourself. Putting your inner thoughts out onto paper is a great way to sort through them and fix whatever needs fixing in your brainspace.

# The Avoidance of Mentally Draining Activities

> > Blaise Pascal: "All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone."
> > 
> > <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6ukJgO8Xrn0" target="_blank">Captain Sinbad: "We don't just need to feel thirsty, we also need something that signals our bodies to get up and go. Dopamine is what spurs you into action, and the more dopamine you have, the more action you will be willing to take."</a>

Many of us know how it feels to be totally void of any sort of drive to to anything at all, as if we are some sort of zombie being chained to our la-z-boy. This feeling is the result of a lack of dopamine production in our brains. Overstimulants such as social media, pornography, drugs, and even stress can change how sensitive we are to our brain's natural dopamine supply. 

If we lack the dopamine necessary to feel a reward for doing a task, the odds that our primitive bodies will attempt to complete that task are astronomically low. This is why many people who struggle with depression have very messy rooms cluttered with things that a mentally-cheerful person would not allow for. Dopamine plays a critical role in our body's ability to stay productive, so it is essential to properly regulate our dopamine levels.

Google search results give plenty of ways to increase dopamine levels (diet and exercise being two amazing ways), but it is equally (if not, even more) important to avoid activities that cause a decline in dopamine levels. The aforementioned uses of pornography and drugs serve as hyperstimulants in our brains and consistantly result in lower levels of motivation. In addition, poor sleep hygiene throws off many people's (EXPECIALLY college students) ability to stay focused and motivated. Even overlooked habits like mindlessly browsing social media can drain us of our energy. I am quite guilty of spending hours on Youtube, in the guise of learning, when in fact I end up actually retaining a miniscule amount of information.
